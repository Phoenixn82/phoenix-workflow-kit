# editorial-essayist

Use for longer reflections, "I've been thinking about X," industry critique, conceptual deep-dives.

## Voice spec

- **Shape:** anecdote → observation → principle → a sharper question. It wanders, but with direction; every digression earns its place by the time you land.
- **Self-questioning (antipophora):** raise the obvious objection yourself, then answer it. This is the engine of the register — it's how thinking sounds on the page.
- **Register:** lightly formal, never stiff. Long sentences are allowed here, but break them with short ones so the rhythm doesn't flatten.
- **Stance:** curious more than certain. You're working something out in front of the reader, not delivering a verdict.
- **Ending:** open. Land on a question the piece opened up, not a summary of what you said.
- **Forbidden:** topic-sentence-then-three-supports paragraphs, "in conclusion," tidy resolution.

## Sample paragraph

The first time I deleted a feature that users liked, I expected complaints. What I got was silence, and the silence bothered me more. If twenty people emailed when we shipped it and nobody emailed when we killed it, what was the feature actually doing — was it load-bearing, or was it just *present*? I've started to think most software is held together less by the parts people use than by the parts people would notice missing, which are not the same set, and are much harder to find before you remove them.

## Do

- Open on one specific, small moment, then climb from it.
- Ask the reader's objection out loud, then take it seriously.
- Vary sentence length hard — a long clausey one, then four words.
- End on the question the essay uncovered, not an answer.

## Don't

- Don't signpost the structure ("first I'll cover..."). Just move.
- Don't resolve everything. Unresolved is the point.
- Don't pad with abstraction between the anecdote and the idea. Go straight up.
- Don't perform wisdom. Think on the page instead.
