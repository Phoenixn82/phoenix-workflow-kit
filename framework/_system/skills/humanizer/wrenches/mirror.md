# mirror

Use whenever the writer has supplied prior samples and wants continuity of their established voice. This wrench wins over every preset mode.

## Voice spec

There is no fixed voice here — the voice is *theirs*. Before rewriting, fingerprint the supplied sample on six axes and write the rewrite inside that fingerprint:

1. **Sentence-length distribution** — do they run long and clausey, or short and clipped? What's their actual variance? Match the *pattern*, not an average.
2. **Paragraph rhythm** — long blocks, or frequent one-liners? Where do they break?
3. **Word register** — Anglo-Saxon and blunt, or Latinate and considered? Profanity, or none? Jargon level?
4. **Paragraph openings** — do they open on the detail, the claim, a question, a story?
5. **Punctuation habits** — em-dash lover? Parenthetical-asides? Semicolons? Sentence fragments? Ellipses? Match the *tics*.
6. **Transition and recurring phrases** — their specific connectors and verbal signatures ("the thing is," "anyway," "look,").

Apply all six silently. **Never describe the fingerprint back to the reader** — the output should simply sound like them. If samples conflict (formal email vs casual blog), match the sample closest to the target piece's context.

## Do

- Build the six-axis fingerprint from the real sample before writing a word.
- Match the writer's *pattern* of variation, not a flattened average.
- Preserve their tics — the parentheticals, the lowercase, the specific filler.
- Pick the sample whose context matches the target piece.

## Don't

- Don't describe the fingerprint to the reader. Apply it invisibly.
- Don't "improve" their voice toward a generic-good default — that erases them.
- Don't average multiple samples into mush. Match the closest one.
- Don't override their habits with this skill's presets. Mirror outranks presets.
